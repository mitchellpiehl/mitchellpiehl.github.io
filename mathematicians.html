Alan Turing

1912-1954

A British mathematician and logician who introduced the concept of the Turing Machine and proposed the Turing Test as a criteria for machine intelligence. His ideas remain central to the philosophical foundations of the field.

Geoffrey Hinton

1946-

A crucial figure in the development of neural networks and deep learning. Has multiple massive contributions to the field of deep learning including co-developing the back propagation algorithm, the foundation for modern neural nets, and winning the 2012 ImageNet competition with a deep convolutional neural network, showing the potential of CNN's in image classification.

Ada Lovelace

1815-1852

19th-century mathematician and the worlds first computer programmer. She worked with Charles baggage on his Analytical Engine and wrote the first algorithm intended to be executed by a machine. Lovelace was the first to envision how computers could go beyond calculation and create art and music.

Charles Babbage

1791-1871

A British mathematician and inventor known as the "father of the computer." He designed the Difference Engine and the more ambitious Analytical Engine, a general-purpose computer. Components of his machines included memory, central processors, and logic which are still core ideas in computers today, even though neither machine was finished in his lifetime.

  • View Person

Image Source: Wikipedia

Claude Shannon

1916-2001

An American mathematician and electrical engineer known as the father of information theory. His paper, A Mathematical Theory of Communication, introduced information entropy, giving birth to information theory. He also introduced the idea of applying Boolean algebra to electrical circuits, making the blueprint for modern computing hardware.

George Boole

1815-1864

19th-century mathematician and logician who introduced Boolean algebra in 1854 in his work titled The Laws of Thought, the system of logic that is the foundation of digital computing. His work helped create programming languages, formal sytems, and modern circuits.

Rene Descartes

1596-1650

A French philosopher and mathematician who introduced dualism. His famous statement "I think, therefore I am" emphasizes his belief that humans are thinking things, and the only things capable of thinking. His seperation of mind and body in dualism helped influence debates of machine learning.

Euclid

300 BCE

Ancient Greek mathematician known as the "father of geometry" due to his work titled Elements. Elements applied a axiomatic approach that is known the standard for formal systems in math, logic, and AI. He was able to form complex truths from foundational assumptions, similar to how AI systems use rules to make logical inferences.

Bertrand Russell

1872-1970

A British philosopher, logician, and mathematician who co-authored Principia Mathematica, which aimed to derive all of mathematics from logical axioms. He is also know for Russell's Paradox which exposed inconsistencies in naive set theory. This exposure then led to more advanced set theory.

Gottlob Frege

1848-1925

A German mathematician and philosopher who founded modern logic by introducing a formal language in his 1879 work titled Begriffssschrift that could express mathematical and logical relationships better than Aristotelian logic. His predicate calculus was very influential to math and computer science by introducing variables, quantifers, and functions.

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

1646-1716

A German polymath who envisioned a universal logical language a machine that could reason. He also invented one of the first mechanical calculators. His ideas that the world could be reduced to calculation are now central to AI.

Thomas Bayes

1701-1761

An 18th century statistician and theologian who developed Bayesian probability. Bayes' Theorem provides a way to update beliefs when new evidence is presented which allows reasoning through uncertainty. This approach is now the backbone of many AI systems.

The Stoics

Around 300 BCE

A school of ancient Greek philosophy that developed an early form of propositional logic, which included truth tables and logical connectives such as if-then, or either-or.

Blaise Pascal

1623-1662

A French mathematician, physicist, and inventor who played an important role in the development of probability theory. His work on the Problem of Points led to the first understandings of expected value and combinatorial reasoning. He also designed and built the Pascaline, one of the earliest mechanical calculators.
